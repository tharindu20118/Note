### ðŸ”· 1. What Are Composite Materials?
A composite material is an advanced, engineered material created by physically combining two or more distinct constituent materials to achieve significantly superior mechanical, thermal, or chemical properties compared to what the individual components could achieve alone.
**Crucial Concept:** The components remain physically distinct and separate at the macroscopic level, but they work together synergistically to bear mechanical loads.

### ðŸ”· 2. The Micro-Structure of a Composite
**Phase 1: The Matrix (The Binder/Continuous Phase)**
*   **Purpose:** The matrix completely surrounds the reinforcement. It holds the fibers together in the desired shape, actively transfers external mechanical loads into the stronger fibers, and protects the fibers from environmental degradation (moisture, chemicals, abrasion).
*   **Types:**
    *   **Polymer Matrix (PMCs):** Most common in industry.
    *   **Metal Matrix (MMCs):** Used in high-stress engine parts.
    *   **Ceramic Matrix (CMCs):** Used in high-temp turbines.
*   **In FRP (Fiberglass) Manufacturing:** The matrix overwhelmingly consists of thermosetting polymers, specifically:
    *   *Polyester resin:* Cost-effective, standard for general water/wastewater tanks.
    *   *Vinyl ester resin:* Superior chemical and corrosion resistance (excellent for Daiki Axis applications).
    *   *Epoxy resin:* Highest mechanical strength, used in aerospace and 3Di high-performance components.

**Phase 2: The Reinforcement (The Strength/Dispersed Phase)**
*   **Purpose:** The reinforcement provides the fundamental tensile strength, stiffness, and structural rigidity. It carries the vast majority of the applied mechanical load.
*   **Types:** Glass fibers (E-glass, S-glass), Carbon fibers, Aramid fibers (Kevlar).
*   **In the Fiberglass Industry:** Glass fiber remains the absolute standard due to its excellent cost-to-strength ratio.

### ðŸ”· 3. Classification of Composites
**A) Based on Matrix Type**
1.  **Polymer Matrix Composites (PMC):** The industry standard. Combines resins with fibers. Used heavily in chemical storage tanks, wastewater treatment plants (Daiki Axis), pipes, and boat hulls.
2.  **Metal Matrix Composites (MMC):** E.g., Aluminum reinforced with silicon carbide (aerospace).
3.  **Ceramic Matrix Composites (CMC):** Used in extreme high-temperature environments.

**B) Based on Reinforcement Form**
1.  **Fiber Reinforced Composites:**
    *   *Continuous Fiber:* Long, unbroken fibers aligned in specific directions.
    *   *Discontinuous Fiber:* Short fibers (chopped).
2.  **Particle Reinforced Composites:** Filled polymers (like concrete or filled plastics).
3.  **Laminar Composites:** Layered structures (like plywood or sandwich panels).

### ðŸ”· 4. Deep Dive: Fiber Reinforced Plastic (FRP)
FRP stands for **Fiber Reinforced Polymer** (or Plastic).
*   **The Blueprint:** Glass Fiber (Strength) + Polymer Resin Matrix (Binder/Protector).
*   **Why does the industry use it?**
    *   *Unmatched Strength-to-Weight Ratio:* Much lighter than steel but offers comparable tensile strength.
    *   *Total Corrosion Resistance:* Unlike metals, FRP does not rust. It resists severe acids, alkalis, and wastewater chemicals.
    *   *Electrical & Thermal Insulation:* Does not conduct electricity easily; retains fluid temperatures well.
    *   *Design Flexibility:* Can be molded into incredibly complex geometric shapes without welding.
*   **Industrial Applications:** Chemical storage tanks, biological wastewater treatment systems (Daiki Axis core product), high-pressure pipes, structural grating, and automotive panels.

### ðŸ”· 5. Manufacturing Processes of FRP (Full Detail)
1.  **Hand Lay-Up (Open Molding):**
    *   *Process:* The most fundamental process. Operators manually place fiber mats into a mold and manually apply resin using rollers and brushes.
    *   *Pros/Cons:* Low capital investment, good for large custom tanks. High labor cost, quality depends on worker skill.
    *   *QC Focus:* Rolling out trapped air is critical.
2.  **Spray-Up:**
    *   *Process:* Operators use a chopper gun that simultaneously cuts continuous roving into short fibers and sprays them alongside catalyzed resin directly onto the mold.
    *   *Use Case:* Bathtubs, boat hulls.
3.  **Filament Winding:**
    *   *Process:* A highly automated process where continuous resin-impregnated fiber rovings are wound under tension over a rotating cylindrical mandrel.
    *   *Use Case:* High-pressure pipes, cylindrical chemical tanks.
4.  **Pultrusion:**
    *   *Process:* A continuous manufacturing process where fibers are pulled through a heated resin bath and then through a heated die to form constant cross-section profiles.
    *   *Use Case:* Structural beams, rods, gratings.
5.  **Vacuum Bagging / Vacuum Resin Infusion:**
    *   *Process:* Operators seal the lay-up inside a plastic bag and apply a vacuum. The vacuum pressure sucks out trapped air and perfectly distributes the resin.
    *   *Benefit:* Ensures an incredibly high fiber-to-resin ratio and eliminates voids.

### ðŸ”· 6. Important Properties of Composites
*   **Mechanical Behavior (Anisotropy):** Unlike steel (isotropic, meaning strength is equal in all directions), composites are usually *anisotropic*. Their mechanical properties depend entirely on the fiber direction. Fibers aligned at 0Â° provide massive tensile strength along that axis.
*   **Chemical Stability:** Exceptionally high resistance to oxidation and chemical attack.
*   **Thermal Dynamics:** Possess low thermal conductivity and highly controlled coefficients of thermal expansion.

### ðŸ”· 7. Key Parameters in Composite Manufacturing (QC Executive Focus)
*If interviewing for the QC role, you MUST mention you know how to monitor these:*
*   **Resin Viscosity:** Too thick = poor fiber wetting. Too thin = runs off vertical surfaces.
*   **Catalyst (Hardener) Ratio:** Usually 1% to 2% MEKP. Incorrect ratios cause the part to under-cure (stay sticky) or over-cure (crack from heat).
*   **Gel Time vs. Curing Time:** Gel time is the exact window the operator has to work the resin before it solidifies. Curing time is how long it takes to reach full mechanical strength.
*   **Exothermic Temperature:** The crosslinking chemical reaction generates heat (exotherm). Uncontrolled heat causes micro-cracking and destroys the part.
*   **Fiber GSM (Grams per Square Meter):** Ensuring the correct weight of glass is used to meet structural design specs.
*   **Thickness Control:** Utilizing ultrasonic thickness gauges to ensure uniformity.
*   **Moisture Control:** Ambient humidity above 70% can introduce water into the resin, completely ruining the chemical crosslinking process.

### ðŸ”· 8. Common Defects in Composites & Root Causes
1.  **Voids (Air Bubbles):** *Cause:* Poor manual rolling, resin mixed too vigorously, insufficient vacuum. *Result:* Massive structural weakness.
2.  **Delamination (Layer Separation):** *Cause:* Contamination between layers (dust/moisture), or applying a new layer after the previous layer has fully cured without proper sanding.
3.  **Dry Spots (Un-wetted fiber):** *Cause:* Not enough resin applied, or resin viscosity too high to penetrate the tight fiber bundles.
4.  **Resin-Rich Areas:** *Cause:* Too much resin applied, pooling in corners. *Result:* Part becomes brittle and heavy.
5.  **Thermal Cracking:** *Cause:* Catalyst ratio too high, resulting in a violent exothermic reaction.
6.  **Uneven Thickness:** *Cause:* Poor workmanship or improper mold alignment.

### ðŸ”· 9. Why Environmental Companies (Daiki Axis) Use Composites
*   Wastewater contains highly corrosive biological and chemical agents; FRP completely resists this corrosion without needing protective paint.
*   Chemical dosing tanks require absolute chemical stability.
*   FRP is exceptionally lightweight, drastically reducing transportation, lifting, and installation costs at remote sites compared to heavy steel tanks.
*   Provides a multi-decade service life with virtually zero maintenance.

### ðŸ”· 10. Composite vs. Conventional Materials (Comparison)
*   **Weight:** FRP is significantly lighter than Steel.
*   **Corrosion:** FRP has very low corrosion; Steel has high corrosion.
*   **Maintenance:** FRP requires low maintenance; Steel requires high maintenance (painting/coating).
*   **Strength-to-Weight:** FRP is High; Steel is Moderate.

### ðŸ”· 11. Important Technical Concepts (Interview Level)
*   **Crosslinking reaction:** The chemical process in thermoset polymers where chains link together to form a rigid structure.
*   **Exothermic curing:** The release of heat during the chemical reaction.
*   **Gel time:** The working time before the resin hardens.
*   **Fiber volume fraction:** The percentage of fiber vs. resin (Higher fiber = stronger part).
*   **Interface bonding:** How well the resin sticks to the fiber.

**Key Technical Features:**

**Operational Focus in the 3Di Plant:**
*   Executing hyper-accurate robotic or manual fiber placement.

### ðŸ§µ 2. Prepregging (The "Pregger" Operation)
**What is Prepreg?**
Prepreg stands for "Pre-impregnated." It refers to continuous fiber reinforcements that the manufacturer has already impregnated with a precisely controlled amount of partially cured (B-Stage) polymer resin.
**Key Characteristics & SOPs:**
*   Offers perfect, factory-controlled resin-to-fiber ratios (eliminating dry spots and resin-rich pooling).
*   **Must** be stored in freezers (usually -18Â°C) to aggressively pause the chemical curing reaction.
*   Requires a highly specific temperature ramp-up and hold cycle to fully cure.
**Operational Risks to Manage:** Exceeding the material's "out-life" at room temperature, which causes the resin to permanently degrade before molding.

### ðŸ§µ 3. Taping / TH (Tape Head) Operations
**What is Taping?**
Taping involves precisely applying these continuous prepreg fiber tapes onto the mold or substrate in highly specific, computer-calculated orientations.
**The Engineering Purpose:**
*   To align the tensile fibers exactly parallel to the anticipated directional loads.
*   To maximize structural efficiency while aggressively reducing unnecessary material weight.
**Critical Quality Controls:**
*   Maintaining strictly uniform tape tension to prevent buckling.
*   Controlling the exact overlap dimensions between adjacent tape strips.
*   Ensuring an absolutely clean, contaminant-free surface to guarantee inter-laminar bonding.
**Common Manufacturing Defects:** Wrinkling of the tape, angular misalignment, and microscopic air entrapment between layers.

### ðŸ­ 4. Lamination & Molding
**Lamination:** The meticulous, layer-by-layer stacking of prepreg tapes or fiber sheets to build up the required structural thickness.
**Molding (Consolidation):** The process of simultaneously applying elevated heat and atmospheric/mechanical pressure to chemically cure the matrix and physically consolidate the layers.

### ðŸ“Š 5. THE COMPLETE GUIDE TO O.E.E. (Overall Equipment Effectiveness)

**What is OEE?**
OEE is the gold standard for measuring manufacturing productivity. It identifies the percentage of manufacturing time that is truly productive. An OEE score of 100% means you are manufacturing only Good Parts, as fast as possible, with no stop time.

**The Formula:**
`OEE = Availability Ã— Performance Ã— Quality`

**Breaking Down the 3 Pillars of OEE (And the "Six Big Losses"):**

1.  **Availability (Is the machine running?)**
    *   *Formula:* `Run Time / Planned Production Time`
    *   *Loss 1: Planned Stops* (Setup time, changeovers, tool adjustments). *Solution: Apply SMED (Single-Minute Exchange of Die).*
    *   *Loss 2: Unplanned Stops* (Equipment breakdowns, material shortages). *Solution: Apply Total Productive Maintenance (TPM).*

2.  **Performance (Is it running as fast as it should?)**
    *   *Formula:* `(Ideal Cycle Time Ã— Total Count) / Run Time`
    *   *Loss 3: Micro-stops* (Jams, blocked sensors, minor cleaning).
    *   *Loss 4: Reduced Speed* (Machine wear, operator inefficiency, bad materials causing slowdowns).

3.  **Quality (Are we making good products?)**
    *   *Formula:* `Good Count / Total Count`
    *   *Loss 5: Production Rejects* (Defects, scrap, rework generated during steady-state production).
    *   *Loss 6: Startup Rejects* (Scrap produced during the warmup or calibration phase after a changeover).
